Past Research projects

Modelling evolution of proteins

University of California, Berkeley, 2010 - Supervisor, Prof. Phillip Geissler

In my first semester at UC Berkeley I investigated the option of a cross-disciplinary project between Computer Science and Chemistry. The particular project I started work on involved applying techniques from machine learning to the problem of protein evolution. In particular, we were considering a lattice model of a protein and wanted to characterise how its folding behaviour varied across the space of possible sequences.

Online detection of transient objects

University of Sydney, Research Assistant, 2010 - Supervisor, Dr. Tara Murphy

One of the major challenges in the Square Kilometre Array project is processing the data collected by the telescope fast enough to be able to notice interesting observations while there is still something to look at. This project was an exploration of methods for storing observations, tracking objects and noticing interesting changes.

Adaptive Supertagging for Faster Parsing

University of Sydney Honours Thesis, 2009 - Supervisor, Dr James R. Curran

My honours thesis investigated adaptive training - using a parser to automatically annotate large amounts of data, which is then used to retrain the parser's supertagger. This leads the supertagger to adapt to the parser, using smaller tag sets while still providing the tags the parser would have used if the sets were larger. This led to speed improvements of between 30% and 86%, with no loss in accuracy. I received a university medal for my work and some of the early results were presented at the Australasian Language Technology Association Workshop 2009.

Predicting long-term dynamics of supercooled liquids

Columbia University, NY, Dec 2008 / Jan 2009 - Supervisor, Prof David R. Reichman

During the summer break after third year I travelled to New York to spend two months working on a research project at Columbia University. I was studying the dynamics of supercooled liquid systems. I implemented a molecular dynamics simulation, two energy minimisation routines and a function to calculate the localised soft modes of a system, all in C++.

Simulating Ion-Surface and Ion-Bulk interactions in Water

Talented Student Program Unit of Study, Semester Two, 2008 - Supervisor, Dr Angus Gray-Weale

I developed a simple simulation of water, written in FORTRAN, to search for a new long-range interaction force. We approximated water as a three dimensional matrix of dipoles that are able to change intensity. Using a Monte-Carlo search heuristic with energy calculated using a fast fourier transform routine we were able to find the likely equilibrium state of the system. By considering the variation in dipole behaviour we aimed to approximate the free energy of the system, hoping to find a variation depending on the proximity of the ion to the surface. While the proposed force was not demonstrated, I did show that the model demonstrates a range of water's known properties and developed tools to make execution and analysis simpler.

Classifying Verb Particle Constructions

Talented Student Program Unit of Study, Semester One, 2008 - Supervisor, Dr James R. Curran

I constructed a system in C and Python that uses hashing and template based aggregation to efficiently store the 24Gb of compressed data in the Google Web1T corpus in a quickly queriable form. This system was used to classify Verb-Particle Constructions by applying a linguistic constituency test to determine whether the verb-particle pair given is in fact a VPC. A paper based on this work was accepted for the 2008 Australian Language Technology Workshop.

Optimal Packing of Hard Spheres of Two Sizes

Talented Student Program Unit of Study, Semester Two, 2007 - Supervisor, Dr Toby Hudson

This project is an extension of the inorganic crystal structures project described below. The work involved writing a Monte-Carlo search heuristic to use currently known binary crystal structures as a starting point for finding the optimal packing configuration for binary hard sphere systems (considering all ratios of radii and ratios of constituents). The results of this worked have subsequently been published in The Journal of Physical Chemistry B, in the United States.

Simulating Protein Aggregation in 3D

Physics Lab, Semester Two, 2007 - Supervisor, Dr S Kuyucak

In this project I investigated the conditions under which aggregation occurs by using Brownian dynamics and the Monte-Carlo search heuristic to simulate the movements of globular proteins. I wrote in C++, and the main challenge was the physics involved, which was simplified by approximations, but complicated by the discrete nature of steps in the simulation.

Inorganic Crystal Structure Data Mining

Vacation Scholarship Project 2006/7 - Supervisor, Dr Toby Hudson

The Inorganic Crystal Database contains descriptions of all inorganic crystals reported, but is not designed for data mining. To convert the data to a more usable form I wrote a simple hotkey macro, a python script and a C program that could extract any set of crystals from the database, filter them, and produce an array of structures containing all properties in appropriate forms. At the end of the project I used the observed separation of atoms in all binary crystals to determine an optimal set of atomic radii (producing a list of radii directly from a large set of experimental observations). The program also included options for analysing particular crystals, for example, producing an output file that could be processed by the visualisation tool xmakemol.

Developing a Write-Skew Detector

Vacation Scholarship Project 2006/7 - Supervisor, Ass. Prof. Alan Fekete

Databases operating under snapshot isolation can experience an error when two records that must satisfy a relation are concurrently edited by transactions that read common records, but do not write common records. In this project I developed a tool in C to detect write-skew errors in logs produced by Berkeley DB. This is achieved by constructing a graph of dependencies between transaction and searching for cycles. When I completed this project I had not learn C formally yet and so the project gave me the opportunity to learn about string manipulation and memory management.

Copper Oxide Semiconductors

Physics Lab, Semester Two, 2006 - Supervisor, Dr Nicholas J. Ekins-Daulkes

I worked with another student, synthesising a semi-conducting material based on cuprous oxide by an electrochemical process. We successfully formed a diode and also measured the light absorption behaviour of the material to assess its potential usefulness in solar cells. While the results indicated it had the right properties to be effective, we did not have time to construct a sample solar cell.

Diffusion Synthesis of Copper and Zinc Based Porous Co-ordination Metal-Organic Framework Materials

Talented Student Program Sustainability Project, Semester One, 2006 - Supervisor, Prof. Cameron J. Kepert

The group, (four students), was synthesising two types of crystals that could store hydrogen. One, the copper based material, had been extensively studied previously, the other, the zinc based one, had only been synthesised once before, and then as a byproduct in a separate experiment. I attempted to synthesise the zinc based material and succeeded in producing crystals that would be capable of storing hydrogen, but their structure was slightly more zinc-rich than expected. The results were particularly exciting because we used a novel diffusion process rather than the solvothermal method often used in the field.

Galactic All Sky Survey Searching Program

Vacation Scholarship Project 2005/6 - Supervisor, Dr Tara Murphy

Usually the search for sources in astronomical data is performed by hand over several months by a physicist. To save this time, I developed a program in python that searched the GASS data for hydrogen clouds by applying a variation of the flood-fill algorithm in 3D. The program produced a list of sources and an annotation file used by the visualisation tool kvis to mark the location of sources in images of the data.
